Introduction

Proper understanding of HIV is crucial in preventing the spread of HIV and AIDS. This report presents findings from the 2016 Malawi Health and Demographic Survey, focusing on HIV-related knowledge among women aged 15-49. The report addresses the primary areas of concern identified in the literature

Variables

Dependent variable :Knowledge of HIV

The analysis adopts the definition of HIV knowledge as articulated by the Joint United Nations Program on HIV and AIDS (UNAIDS). According to this definition, HIV prevention knowledge encompasses the following:

  • Understanding that the use of condoms and limiting sexual intercourse to one uninfected partner are both HIV prevention methods.

  • Recognizing that a person who appears healthy can still be HIV positive.

  • Rejecting the two prevalent local misconceptions about HIV transmission:

  • Believing HIV cannot be transmitted via mosquito bites.

  • Thinking HIV cannot be transmitted through supernatural means

Explanatory variables

From review, the following explanatory variables were included:

  • Age

  • Rerigion

  • Ethinicity

  • Highest education

  • Wealth index

  • Marital status

  mutate(
    HIV_knowledge = case_when(

        Healthy_looking_person_can_have_HIV == "yes" &

      TRUE ~ "No"
    ),
    HIV_knowledge = as.factor(HIV_knowledge)
  )

# Chart 3+ : Distribution of Respondents who had knowledge of HIV----
# List of categories
categories <- c("Region", "Highest_educational_level", "Religion", "Ethnicity", "Wealth_index_combined", "Current_marital_status")

# Create an empty list to store plots
plots_list <- list()

# Generate plots for each category and store them in the list
for (category in categories) {
  combined_chart_data <- mw_hiv_raw %>%
    group_by(!!sym(category)) %>%
    summarise(count_yes = sum(HIV_knowledge == "Yes"),
              total = n(),
              percentage_yes = count_yes / total)
  
  p3 <- ggplot(combined_chart_data, aes(x = reorder(!!sym(category), -percentage_yes), y = percentage_yes, fill = !!sym(category))) +
    geom_bar(stat = "identity") +
    geom_text(aes(label = scales::percent(percentage_yes, accuracy = 1)), position = position_stack(vjust = 0.5), color = "white", fontface = "bold") +
    labs(title = paste("HIV Knowledge by", category),
         y = "% of respondents with HIV-Knowledge",
         x = "Respondent Category") +
    theme_minimal() +
    theme(legend.position = "none", axis.text.x = element_text(angle = 45, hjust = 1)) +
    scale_y_continuous(labels = scales::percent_format()) +
    expand_limits(y = c(min(combined_chart_data$percentage_yes), max(combined_chart_data$percentage_yes))) 
  
  plots_list[[category]] <- p3
}

Results

The study focused on the descriptive analysis to show the variation of HIV knowledge by different determinant

Respondents in the survey

A total of 24,562 women, aged between 15 and 49, were surveyed about their HIV-related knowledge.

pop_pyramid_plot
Figure 1: Age Distribution of surveyed women.

Distribution of HIV-Related Knoweledge

The average age of respondents was 28 years, while the median age stood at 27 years. Figure 1 below illustrates this distribution. Notably, the median age was marginally higher among women who had prior information related to HIV.

p1
Figure 2: Box plot for HIV knowledge.

Table 1 below displays the distribution of the response variable across 5-year age groups. Among the surveyed women, adolescent girls aged 15-19 had the lowest knowledge of HIV at 52%, while women in the 24-29 and 30-34 year groups exhibited the highest knowledge at 63%

How does HIV-related information vary by region?

Figure 3 below indicates that the Southern region had the highest prior HIV knowledge at 61%, while the Northern region recorded the lowest at 55%

plots_list[["Region"]]
Figure 3: HIV Knowledge by Region.

How does HIV-related information vary by Highest education ?

Figure 4 below reveals that women with higher education had the highest prior HIV knowledge at 71%, whereas those with no education recorded the lowest at 49%.

plots_list[["Highest_educational_level"]]
Figure 4: HIV Knowledge by Highest education level.

How does HIV-related information vary by religion?

Figure 5 below indicates that women who were Anglican had higher prior HIV knowledge, while women from other religions had a knowledge rate of 50%.

plots_list[["Religion"]]
Figure 5:HIV Knowledge by Religion.

How does HIV-related information vary by Ethnicity ?

Figure 6 below reveals that women of Nyanja ethnicity had a higher prior knowledge of HIV at 73%, whereas those from the Tumbuka ethnicity recorded a lower rate at 50%.

plots_list[["Ethnicity"]]
Figure 6: HIV Knowledge by Ethinicity.

How does HIV-related information vary by Wealth Index ?

Figure 7 below reveals that women from the rich wealth index had a higher prior knowledge of HIV at 66%, whereas those from the poorest wealth index recorded a lower rate at 51%.

plots_list[["Wealth_index_combined"]]

How does HIV-related information vary by Marital status ?

Figure 8 below reveals that widowed women had the highest knowledge at 61%, while those who were never in a union recorded a lower rate at 56%

Conclusion

This descriptive analysis has revealed that comprehensive knowledge about HIV in Malawi varies across different determinants. A full inferential study should be undertaken to identify the factors that affect HIV knowledge in Malawi.
